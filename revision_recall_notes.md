## <a id="anti-defection-law-schedule-x"></a>1. Anti-Defection Law (Schedule X)

### ðŸ“Œ Basics & Origins
**Q: Which Constitutional Amendment introduced the Anti-Defection Law?**
> **52nd Amendment Act, 1985**.

**Q: Which Schedule was added by this amendment?**
> **10th Schedule**.

**Q: What phrase was used to describe political defection in the 1960s?**
> "Aya Ram, Gaya Ram".

### ðŸš« Grounds for Disqualification
**Q: When is a member of a Political Party disqualified?**
> 1. Voluntarily **gives up membership**.
> 2. Votes/Abstains contrary to the **Whip** (without prior permission).

**Q: When does an Independent Member get disqualified?**
> If they join **any political party** *after* the election.

**Q: When does a Nominated Member get disqualified?**
> If they join a political party **after 6 months** of taking their seat.

### âš–ï¸ Authority & Exceptions
**Q: Who decides the disqualification of a member?**
> The **Presiding Officer** of the House (Speaker/Chairman).

**Q: What are the two major exceptions to disqualification?**
> 1. **Merger:** If **2/3rds** of members merge with another party.
> 2. **Presiding Officer:** If Speaker/Deputy Speaker resigns from party to remain neutral.

**Q: Can the courts interfere in the Speaker's decision? (Case Law)**
> **Kihoto Hollohan Case (1992):**
> *   Speaker acts as a **Tribunal**.
> *   Decision is subject to **Judicial Review** (on grounds of malafide/perversity), but only *after* the decision is made.

## <a id="the-whip-system"></a>2. The Whip System

### ðŸ” Concept & Types
**Q: Is the office of 'Whip' mentioned in the Constitution?**
> **No.** It is a convention based on the **British** system.

**Q: What is the main objective of a Whip?**
> Ensure party discipline and attendance.

**Q: What are the three types of Whips?**
> 1. **One-line:** Information only (non-binding).
> 2. **Two-line:** Must be present during voting.
> 3. **Three-line:** Strict instruction to **be present AND vote** a certain way. (Violation = Disqualification).

## <a id="reforms--committees"></a>3. Reforms & Committees

### ðŸ›ï¸ Key Committees
**Q: What did the Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990) recommend regarding disqualification authority?**
> Disqualification should be decided by the **President/Governor** (on advice of Election Commission), NOT the Speaker.

**Q: What was the Supreme Court's suggestion in the Keisham Meghachandra Case (2020)?**
> *   Speaker should decide cases within **3 months**.
> *   Suggested creating an **Independent Tribunal** (headed by retired SC judge) to decide disqualification.

**Q: What is the "Page Committee"?**
> A committee formed by a forum of speakers to suggest reforms for the Speaker's office.

## <a id="parliamentary-privileges-article-105"></a>4. Parliamentary Privileges (Article 105)

### ðŸ›¡ï¸ Definition & Scope
**Q: Which Article deals with Parliamentary Privileges?**
> **Article 105** (Parliament) and **Article 194** (State Legislature).

**Q: Have these privileges been codified into law?**
> **No.** They are based on conventions of the **British House of Commons**.

**Q: Why are privileges not codified?**
> Legislators argue it would subject privileges to **Fundamental Rights** and judicial scrutiny (sovereignty issue).

### ðŸ‘¥ Collective vs. Individual Privileges
**Q: What are "Collective Privileges"? (Belong to the House)**
> 1. Right to **publish reports/debates** (and prohibit others).
> 2. Exclude **strangers** (Secret Sitting).
> 3. Punish for **Contempt** or Breach of Privilege.
> 4. Make rules for its own procedure.

**Q: What is a "Secret Sitting"?**
> *   Only members allowed.
> *   Decided by the **Presiding Officer**.
> *   Disclosure of proceedings is a gross breach of privilege.

**Q: What is Article 104?**
> Penalty for sitting/voting in the House **without taking oath**.
