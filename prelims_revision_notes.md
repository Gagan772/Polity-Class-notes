## <a id="anti-defection-law-schedule-x"></a>Anti-Defection Law (Schedule X)

### Definition
‚Ä¢ **Defection** = MP/MLA changes political party OR independent MP/MLA joins party **post-election**
‚Ä¢ **Example:** BJP MP joins Congress post-election

### Historical Context
‚Ä¢ Started post-**1967** in India
‚Ä¢ Termed as **"Aya Ram, Gaya Ram"**
‚Ä¢ Parliament enacted **52nd Amendment 1985** ‚Üí Added **Tenth Schedule**

### Grounds for Disqualification

**1. Members of Political Parties:**
‚Ä¢ Voluntarily **gives up membership**
‚Ä¢ **Abstains/votes** contrary to party **whip direction** without prior permission

**2. Independent Members:**
‚Ä¢ Joins **any political party** after election

**3. Nominated Members:**
‚Ä¢ Joins any political party **after expiry of 6 months** from date of taking seat

### Final Authority
‚Ä¢ **Speaker/Chairman** decides disqualification of member

## <a id="52nd-amendment-act-1985"></a>52nd Amendment Act, 1985

### Objectives
‚Ä¢ Combat **"evil of political defections"**
‚Ä¢ Prevent undermining of **democracy foundations**
‚Ä¢ Ensure stability of political system

### Provisions
‚Ä¢ **Elected members** of Parliament/State Legislature set up by political party ‚Üí disqualified if:
  - Voluntarily **relinquishes membership**
  - Votes/abstains contrary to **party direction**
  - Gets **expelled** from party

‚Ä¢ **Nominated member** (if member of party at time of taking seat OR becomes member within 6 months) ‚Üí disqualified on same grounds

‚Ä¢ **Independent member** ‚Üí disqualified if joins any party after election

‚Ä¢ **Nominated member** (not member of any party) ‚Üí disqualified if joins party **after 6 months** from taking seat

### Decision Authority
‚Ä¢ **Presiding Officer** determines disqualification
‚Ä¢ If presiding officer himself ‚Üí decided by **member elected by House**

## <a id="exceptions-in-schedule-x"></a>Exceptions in Schedule X

### When Disqualification Does NOT Apply

**1. Merger of Parties:**
‚Ä¢ If **2/3rds** of MP/MLAs merge with another party ‚Üí NOT defection
‚Ä¢ Originally **1/3rd** ‚Üí Changed by **91st Amendment Act**

**2. Presiding Officers:**
‚Ä¢ **Speaker, Deputy Speaker, Deputy Chairman** ‚Üí NOT covered under Tenth Schedule
‚Ä¢ Can **resign from party** ‚Üí remain **independent** for rest of term
‚Ä¢ **Cannot join** any other party

### Judicial Interference
‚Ä¢ Schedule X says judiciary **barred from interfering**
‚Ä¢ **Kihoto Hollohan Case** ‚Üí Supreme Court held:
  - Can interfere and give decisions in Schedule X matters
  - Speaker's decision subject to judicial review

## <a id="office-of-speaker-and-schedule-x"></a>Office of Speaker and Schedule X

### Issues
‚Ä¢ Speakers accused of acting in **favor of political parties** (biased decision-making)

### Key Judgments

**1. Kihoto Holohan Case:**
‚Ä¢ Speaker acts as **quasi-judicial authority**
‚Ä¢ Decision can be **reviewed by Court** if malafide intention

**2. Keisham Meghchand Case (2020):**
‚Ä¢ Speaker acts as **quasi-judicial authority**
‚Ä¢ Decision reviewable if malafide

### Supreme Court Suggestions (Keisham Meghachandra Case)
‚Ä¢ Speaker should dispose case in **3 months**
‚Ä¢ Power should be given to **tribunal** consisting of:
  - Retired SC judges, OR
  - Chief Justice of High Court, OR
  - President/Governor
‚Ä¢ Political parties should **not issue unnecessary whips**

## <a id="whip-system"></a>Whip System

### Definition & Origin
‚Ä¢ **Whip** = Directions/orders by political party to members in house
‚Ä¢ **British concept** ‚Üí ensures lawmakers abide by party high command directions
‚Ä¢ **NOT a constitutional tool** (not mentioned in Constitution)

### Objective
‚Ä¢ Ensure **uniformity in decisions** by party members in house
‚Ä¢ Deviation ‚Üí disqualification from party ‚Üí ultimately disqualification from house
‚Ä¢ Party's high command discretion whether to take action

### Types of Whips

**1. One-line Whip:**
‚Ä¢ Only **information** sent
‚Ä¢ MP/MLA can **decide on their own**

**2. Two-line Whip:**
‚Ä¢ MP/MLA acts on direction during voting
‚Ä¢ **No specific instructions** issued

**3. Three-line Whip:**
‚Ä¢ **Directions issued** ‚Üí members should vote accordingly
‚Ä¢ **Strictest** type

### Functions
‚Ä¢ Ensure **smooth functioning** of house
‚Ä¢ Ensure **discipline** in house

### Democratic Concerns
‚Ä¢ Whips may be **against democratic decision-making** of MP/MLAs
‚Ä¢ Usage should be **limited to certain cases**

### International Scenario

**UK & USA:**
‚Ä¢ Use whip practice
‚Ä¢ Also have **free voting system** ‚Üí MPs can vote according to wish

**UK Specific:**
‚Ä¢ If MP disqualified due to three-line whip ‚Üí can continue as **independent member**

### Conclusion
‚Ä¢ Whip = **effective tool** for smooth and efficient Parliament functioning

## <a id="importance-of-anti-defection-laws"></a>Importance of Anti-Defection Laws

**1. Ensure Stability:**
‚Ä¢ Provides stability to government
‚Ä¢ Prevents shifts of party allegiance

**2. Increases Faith:**
‚Ä¢ Ensures candidates elected with party support remain **loyal to party policies**
‚Ä¢ Follow party manifestos

**3. Party Discipline:**
‚Ä¢ Promotes party discipline

**4. Increases Public Trust:**
‚Ä¢ Prevents **breach of trust** due to defection
‚Ä¢ Ensures stability

**5. Reduced Horse-Trading:**
‚Ä¢ Curbs **power of money** after election within house
‚Ä¢ Horse-trading and unstable government caused by money power/bribery were rampant in cross-voting

**6. Reduce Money Power:**
‚Ä¢ Succeeded in reducing money power within house voting
‚Ä¢ For passing legislation and government survival

### Overall Purpose
‚Ä¢ Maintain **rule of law**
‚Ä¢ Reduce **political corruption and bribery**
‚Ä¢ Restricts individual decision-making
‚Ä¢ Mandates faithful adherence to party whip directions
‚Ä¢ Solves **money-muscle power** issue

## <a id="issues-with-anti-defection-law"></a>Issues with Anti-Defection Law

**1. Affects Independence of MPs:**
‚Ä¢ Violates **freedom of speech**
‚Ä¢ Can't take independent stand on issues
‚Ä¢ **Example:** USA - 7 Republican legislators voted to impeach Trump, faced no consequences

**2. Injustice to Constituencies:**
‚Ä¢ MPs = people's representatives
‚Ä¢ Should air grievances and speak for constituency requirements

**3. Accountability vs Efficiency:**
‚Ä¢ Dr. B.R. Ambedkar favored **stability and accountability** over efficiency
‚Ä¢ Adopted parliamentary form
‚Ä¢ Weakening of anti-defection law provides **neither efficiency nor stability**

**4. Limited Deliberations:**
‚Ä¢ Reduced quality of Parliamentary debates/deliberations
‚Ä¢ May generate **faulty legislation** with loopholes
‚Ä¢ Paves way for **delegated legislation**
‚Ä¢ Renders Parliament ineffective

**5. Misuse by Speaker:**
‚Ä¢ False dissent by speaker
‚Ä¢ **Examples:** Arunachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand cases

**6. Role Undermined:**
‚Ä¢ Legislators = agents of people
‚Ä¢ Should participate in important legislations
‚Ä¢ Voting only on party lines **hinders primary goals**

**7. Emerging Loopholes:**
‚Ä¢ **Example:** Post Karnataka elections
‚Ä¢ MLAs made to resign ‚Üí artificially weaken numerical strength
‚Ä¢ By-polls held to topple government
‚Ä¢ Discretion lies with speaker ‚Üí adds complexity

**8. No Incentives:**
‚Ä¢ Lack of accountability and speech limit
‚Ä¢ MPs/MLAs find **no incentives** to research policies and find solutions

**9. No Barrier for Wholesale Defection:**
‚Ä¢ Law prohibits **retail defection**
‚Ä¢ **Not effective** for wholesale defection of legislators

**10. Issues with Merger Provisions:**
‚Ä¢ Exception based on **number of members** (2/3rds agreed)
‚Ä¢ **Not based on reason** behind defection

**11. Absence of Time Limit:**
‚Ä¢ **No time limit** for Presiding Officers to decide disqualification
‚Ä¢ Courts can intervene **only after decision made**
‚Ä¢ **Example:** Manipur minister case
  - Speaker didn't decide for 3 years
  - SC dismissed minister
  - Court held: ideally decide within **3 months**

## <a id="reforms--committees"></a>Reforms & Committees

### Way Forward
‚Ä¢ Anti-defection law should be improved to accommodate **SC judgments**

### Reform Committees

**1. Dinesh Goswami Committee on Electoral Reforms (1990):**
‚Ä¢ **President/Governor** should decide defection
‚Ä¢ **NOT presiding officer**

**2. Law Commission Report (170th Report, 1999):**
‚Ä¢ Political parties should **limit issuance of whips**
‚Ä¢ Issue whips only when **government in danger**

**3. Election Commission Constitution Review Commission (2002):**
‚Ä¢ Decisions under Tenth Schedule by **President/Governor**
‚Ä¢ On **binding advice** of Election Commission

### Other Issues with Office of Speaker
‚Ä¢ **Certification of Money Bill** ‚Üí Example: Aadhaar Bill Case
‚Ä¢ **Misuse of power** in money bills
‚Ä¢ **Partisan nature** (acting in party's favor)

### Suggestions for Office of Speaker

**1. Continue as Speaker for Life:**
‚Ä¢ **"Once a speaker, always a speaker"**

**2. Resign from Party:**
‚Ä¢ Should resign from party membership post-election to speaker seat

**3. Money Bill Certification:**
‚Ä¢ Committee of **two senior MPs** can assist speaker
‚Ä¢ In finalizing money bill
‚Ä¢ **Practiced in UK**

### Page Committee
‚Ä¢ Formed by **forum of speakers**
‚Ä¢ Suggestion: Once elected as speaker ‚Üí should be given **second term uncontested** from constituency

## <a id="parliamentary-privileges-article-105"></a>Parliamentary Privileges (Article 105)

### Definition
‚Ä¢ **Special rights, immunities, exemptions** enjoyed by:
  - Two Houses of Parliament
  - Their committees
  - Their members
‚Ä¢ To ensure **effective and efficient functioning**

### Origin
‚Ä¢ Borrowed from **British system**
‚Ä¢ Ensures **protection of lawmakers**

### Legal Status
‚Ä¢ **Article 105 & 194** ‚Üí powers, privileges, immunities to be defined by Parliament
‚Ä¢ **No law enacted so far** in this respect

### Evolution

**1. In British Parliament:**
‚Ä¢ Prevent **arrest and harassment** of legislators in House of Commons
‚Ä¢ When they criticized the **crown**

**2. The Indian Story:**
‚Ä¢ Founding fathers provided parliamentarians **absolute freedom of speech** in house
‚Ä¢ Citizens' freedom was **restricted**
‚Ä¢ SC upheld decision ‚Üí respect for legislators (mostly freedom fighters)

**3. Powers to Codify:**
‚Ä¢ Founding fathers left decision to codify on **wisdom of Parliament**
‚Ä¢ Current legislators felt codification would **take away sovereignty**
‚Ä¢ Argued they don't constitute civil servants
‚Ä¢ **Struck down in P.V. Narasimha Rao Case**

### Types of Privileges

**1. Individual Privileges**
**2. Collective Privileges**

### Collective Privileges

‚Ä¢ For maintaining **dignity of house**
‚Ä¢ Belong to **House of Parliament collectively**

**Powers Include:**

**1. Publish Reports:**
‚Ä¢ Right to **publish reports, debates, proceedings**
‚Ä¢ Right to **prohibit others** from publishing same

**2. Exclude Strangers:**
‚Ä¢ Can exclude strangers from proceedings
‚Ä¢ Hold **secret sittings** for important matters

**3. Make Rules:**
‚Ä¢ Regulate **own procedure**
‚Ä¢ Conduct of business
‚Ä¢ Adjudicate upon matters

**4. Punish for Breach:**
‚Ä¢ Can punish **members and outsiders**
‚Ä¢ For breach of privileges or contempt
‚Ä¢ By **reprimand, admonition, imprisonment**
‚Ä¢ Also **suspension/expulsion** (for members)

### Powers in Breach of Privileges
‚Ä¢ Parliament can punish members and outsiders for **contempt**
