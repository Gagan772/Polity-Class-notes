## üõë Anti-Defection Law (Schedule X - 1985)

<details>
<summary><strong>1. What defines "defection" for different types of members?</strong></summary>

- **Political Party Member:**
  1. Voluntarily gives up membership.
  2. Votes/Abstains contrary to party **Whip** (without prior permission).
- **Independent Member:** Joins *any* political party after election.
- **Nominated Member:** Joins a political party *after* **6 months** of taking their seat.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. Who is the deciding authority for disqualification?</strong></summary>

- The **Speaker** or **Chairman** of the House.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. What are the exceptions to disqualification?</strong></summary>

- **Merger:** If **2/3rds** of members merge with another party.
- **Presiding Officer:** Speaker/Deputy Speaker can resign from their party to remain neutral without disqualification.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. Can the courts interfere in Anti-Defection decisions? (Case Law)</strong></summary>

- **Kihoto Hollohan Case (1992):** SC ruled that the Speaker acts as a **Tribunal**.
- Their decision **IS subject to judicial review** (on grounds of malafide, etc.), but only *after* the decision is made.
</details>

## üìú The Whip System

<details>
<summary><strong>1. Is the "Whip" mentioned in the Constitution?</strong></summary>

- **No**. It is a convention borrowed from the **British** system.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. What are the three types of Whips?</strong></summary>

1.  **One-line Whip:** Information only; member can decide.
2.  **Two-line Whip:** Presence required during voting; no specific instruction on how to vote.
3.  **Three-line Whip:** Strict order to be present **AND vote** according to party line. Violation leads to disqualification.
</details>

## ‚öñÔ∏è Committees & Reforms

<details>
<summary><strong>1. What did the Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990) suggest?</strong></summary>

- Disqualification should be decided by the **President/Governor** (on advice of Election Commission), NOT the Speaker.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. What was the Supreme Court's suggestion in the Keisham Meghachandra Case (2020)?</strong></summary>

- Speakers should decide cases within **3 months**.
- Suggested an **independent tribunal** (retired judges) to decide disqualification instead of the Speaker to ensure neutrality.
</details>

## üõ°Ô∏è Parliamentary Privileges (Article 105)

<details>
<summary><strong>1. What are Parliamentary Privileges and are they codified?</strong></summary>

- Special rights/immunities for MPs and the House.
- **Not codified** by any law yet (Parliament has the power but hasn't used it).
- Based on **British House of Commons** conventions.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. What is Article 104?</strong></summary>

- Penalty for sitting and voting in the House **without taking the oath**.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. What are the "Collective Privileges" of the House?</strong></summary>

1.  Right to **publish** debates/reports (and prohibit others).
2.  Exclude **strangers** (Secret Sitting).
3.  Make rules for its own procedure.
4.  **Punish** members/outsiders for Breach of Privilege or Contempt.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. What is a "Secret Sitting"?</strong></summary>

- Only members are allowed.
- Decided by the **Presiding Officer**.
- Proceedings and reports are **not** made public.
</details>
